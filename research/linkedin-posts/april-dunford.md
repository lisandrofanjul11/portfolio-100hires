## Post 1: The 4 Prerequisites of B2B Positioning

**Core idea:** Never rush into positioning work. If you don't align your team on four foundational decisions *before* starting, you will end up debating mid-way through and redoing your work.

### The 4 Decisions to Make Before Positioning
1. **Is now the right time?** You need enough actual sales experience to know how prospects make decisions and what separates a "good-fit" from a "bad-fit" customer. If you lack this data, build a temporary positioning thesis and wait until you've closed more deals.
2. **Who is this positioning for?** Positioning for customers is fundamentally different from positioning for investors or employees. If half your team is writing an investor pitch and the other half a sales pitch, the messaging will fail.
3. **What exactly are we positioning?** Are you positioning the entire company, a single product, or a bundle/suite of products? Define the scope upfront so it aligns strictly with your Go-To-Market (GTM) strategy.
4. **Which persona are you positioning for?** When you define "value," you must be clear on *who* perceives that value. Keep your internal teams focused on the "deal champion" (the person evaluating the tool) rather than getting distracted by secondary personas.

## Post 2: Vendor Confidence vs. Buyer Confidence

**Core idea:** B2B deals don't stall because buyers don't trust the vendor; they stall because buyers don't trust their own ability to make a good decision. You win not by selling harder, but by helping customers buy confidently. (Insights drawn from "The Framemaking Sale").

### The "Vendor Confidence" Trap
Vendors mistakenly try to inspire confidence by talking about themselves to prove they are a safe choice:
*   "Trusted by 80% of the Fortune 500"
*   "We are the industry leader"
*   "Trusted by [insert impressive logo here]"

### The "Buyer Confidence" Reality
Customers aren't as worried about *your* credibility as they are overwhelmed by their own internal decision-making process. To move a deal forward, you need to help them answer:
*   "How confident are we that **WE** have thoroughly explored the alternatives?"
*   "How confident are we that **WE** can actually implement this chosen solution?"
*   "How confident are we that **WE** have made the best choice for our company?"

### The Tactical Shift
Stop trying to change the customer's perception of your product. Instead, strengthen their self-perception by providing clear frameworks to help them navigate internal consensus, evaluate options, and justify their decision.
