## Post 1: Homepage Messaging: Write for the Champion, Not the CEO

**Core idea:** B2B SaaS homepages fail when they strip out product features to focus on generic executive outcomes (like "boost ROI"). You must write for the end-user evaluating the tool, not the executive approving the budget.

### The Problem with "Outcome" Copy
Founders are often afraid of executives killing deals, so they write homepages filled with generic outcomes (e.g., "increase your revenue", "modernize your business"). 
The reality: The executive/CEO is too busy to shop for software and will never read the homepage. 

### The Solution: Arm the Champion
The person actually reading the homepage is the "Champion" (a lower-level employee tasked with evaluating tools). Generic ROI claims do not help them make a decision because every company says the same thing. 
*   **What you should do:** Explain exactly **HOW** your product solves their specific problem, especially compared to alternative solutions. Give them the ammunition they need to defend the product internally.

### The One Exception
If you are targeting very small companies where the CEO is involved in the day-to-day operations, they might read the homepage. However, they will *still* be trying to answer the same question as a Champion: "Why should I choose you over other solutions?" Therefore, the strategy remains the same: explain how the product actually works.

## Post 2: Functional vs. Business Outcomes in B2B SaaS

**Core idea:** Stop promising impossible business outcomes (like "increased revenue"). B2B software is "workflow software," so you should only market the functional workflow improvements your product actually guarantees.

### The Problem with "Selling the Outcome"
Marketers are trained to sell business outcomes ("get more customers," "increase revenue"). The problem is that no single product can guarantee a business-level outcome. Winning customers depends on dozens of interlocking factors (pricing, sales execution, market timing, product readiness, etc.) that your software doesn't control. Promising ROI is often a lie.

### The "Workflow Software" Reality
B2B software is ultimately workflow software—it helps workers do their jobs better. 
*   **Bad Example:** "Our CRM will help you win more customers." (False, winning customers requires a good product, sales team, etc.)
*   **Good Example:** "Our CRM will help your team stay organized, do accurate forecasting, and track all customer touchpoints." (True, these are functional guarantees).

### The Golden Rule of B2B Copywriting
If you would feel uncomfortable putting an outcome (like "increase revenue by 20%") into a legally binding contract as a guaranteed result, you should avoid saying it in your marketing materials. Promise only what your product can actually guarantee.
